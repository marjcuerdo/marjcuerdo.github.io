  • Death & Rebirth in Platformer Games

    Book Chapter


    CHI Paper


    Edward F. Melcer and Marjorie Ann M. Cuerdo. (2020). “Death & Rebirth in Platformer Games”. In Game User Experience and Player-Centered Design. Springer.

    Marjorie Ann Cuerdo and Edward Melcer. (2020). “’I’ll Be Back’: A Taxonomy of Death and Rebirth in Platformer Video Games”. In Extended Abstracts of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems. CHI '20, Honolulu, HI, USA. ACM.

    Advised by Edward Melcer.

    Abstract. At the heart of failure in many game genres is player death. This chapter presents our exploration into the space of player death and rebirth through the creation of a generalized taxonomy of death in platformer games---one of the genres that features player death and respawning most heavily. In order to create this taxonomy, we collected and catalogued examples of respawning mechanics from 62 recent platformer games released on the digital distribution platform Steam after January 2018. Games selected varied equally across positive, mixed, and negative overall reviews in order to provide a broader range of mechanics, both good and bad. We observed gameplays of each individual game and noted the processes of death and rebirth respectively. A grounded theory approach was then employed to develop the taxonomy of game death and respawning, resulting in five notable dimensions: 1) death conditions, 2) respawn locations, 3) changes to player progress, 4) obstacles, and 5) aesthetics. Finally, we argue how the different dimensions and mechanics highlighted in our taxonomy have substantial implications for key aspects of player experience such as flow and enjoyment, and discuss how they could be used to improve the effectiveness of related techniques such as dynamic difficulty adjustment.

    Professor Melcer and I worked on this project together. We had iterative discussions on data obtained from observations and coding to develop and write about the taxonomy. I'm currently extending the work by developing a platformer game where I set different conditions for a death and rebirth mechanic, and gather data on how game flow, difficulty, and player reception are affected.

  • Evolving Child-Computer Interaction: Mobile-based Interaction Design and User Testing

    CHI Paper


    Cynthia Putnam, Melisa Puthenmadom, Marjorie Ann Cuerdo, Wanshu Wang, and Nathan Paul. (2020). “Adaptation of the System Usability Scale for User Testing with Children”. In Extended Abstracts of the 2020 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems. CHI '20, Honolulu, HI, USA. ACM.

    In Review: Cynthia Putnam, Melisa Puthenmadom, Marjorie Ann Cuerdo, Wanshu Wang, and Nathan Paul. “Benchmarking Usability and Learnability of Comparable Interface and Interaction Design Patterns of Mobile Apps Designed for Children: A Pilot Study of Methods”.

    Advised by Cynthia Putnam.

    Abstract. While it is highly likely that children under the age of 12 have used a mobile device in the U.S., there is a paucity of information to guide interaction designers and usability experts about how to design and test their mobile apps with children. In this work (currently a pilot study), I have conducted user testing of mobile-based apps aimed at computational thinking with two different age groups (7-8 and 9-11). The pilot study will lay a foundation for an envisioned larger project that will result in developmental-focused interaction design guidelines for mobile apps; future work will include creating and testing the efficacy of a tool for designers to access the guideline libraries. This study also contributes to the knowledgebase of user testing with children; specifically, we are working to modify and adapt the System Usablity Scale (SUS) for these two groups.

    There were 3 other RAs involved in this project. As an RA, I moderated in-lab studies and assisted in qualitative analysis and writing. We are currently working on more conference paper submissions in relation to other aspects of this project.

  • Games User Research (GUR) Methods

    Ghostbusters


    Tethered


    Captain's Hold


    My Games User Research (GUR) group project conducted GUR methods for the game Ghostbusters (2016) and early builds of two senior undergrad Game Development capstone projects (Tethered and Captain's Hold). We utilized DePaul's Software Observation and Usability Laboratory (SOUL) to do the studies. I conducted a competitive review, heuristic evaluations, usability tests, and playtests.

    For the above projects, I was part of 2-person (Ghostbusters and Captain's Hold) and 3-person (Tethered) teams where the workload for conducting evaluations, analysis, and writing was split equally.

  • Music for Stories

    Report


    My UX Research Methods group project was an exploratory study about the process content creators go through to add music and additional auditory material to add to their material. We conducted observations, interviews, and surveys throughout this term-long project. This is the final report containing details about each stage of the process and our implications for design.
    Includes: study protocols, statistical survey results, personas and scenarios

    I was part of a 4-person group where the workload for obtaining data was split equally. I did a significant part of the qualitative and qualitative analysis and writing.
